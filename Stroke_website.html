Trouble Walking

Stumbling or sudden dizziness, loss of balance or coordination

Trouble speaking and understanding

One may experience confusion, a slurring of words, or difficulty understanding someone else who is talking.

Trouble Seeing

Blurred or blackened vision in one or both eyes, or seeing double.

Headache

A sudden, severe headache, sometimes accompanied by vomiting, dizziness, or altered consciousness.

Thrombotic Stroke

Occurs when a blood clot forms in one of the arteries that supply blood to your brain.

Embolic stroke

Occurs when a blood clot or other debris forms away from your brain — commonly in your heart — and is swept through your bloodstream to lodge in narrower brain arteries.

Hemorrhagic stroke

Occurs when a blood vessel in your brain leaks or ruptures.

Transient ischemic attack (TIA)

Also called a ministroke; a TIA is a temporary decrease in blood supply to part of your brain, often lasting less than five minutes.

Emergency Treatment with Medications

If caught in time, the damaging effects of a clot can be reduced using medications. Aspirin or Clot Busting Medications (Intravenous injection of tissue plasminogen activator)

Medications delivered to the brain

Sometimes a thin tube (catheter) can be inserted through an artery in your groin and thread it to your brain, releasing medications directly into the area where the stroke is occurring.

Mechanical clot removal

Doctors sometimes use a catheter to maneuver a tiny device into your brain to physically break up or remove the clot.
